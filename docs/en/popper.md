# Popper — Seek to falsify, not to confirm

## Why this encoding?

Confirmation bias is the default failure mode. We seek data that confirms what we already believe, interpret the absence of refutation as proof, and accumulate "successes" that have never been stress-tested. Popper encodes the inverse operation: precisely formulate what would invalidate a hypothesis, and go looking for that case first. Without this encoding, product and strategic hypotheses look like theses — they accumulate confirmations and ignore contrary signals.

## What the method corrects

When someone says "the data confirms that...", the method asks: what would have allowed refutation? If no one can answer, the hypothesis isn't a hypothesis — it's a belief. It also corrects "ad hoc amendments": when a theory is modified at each counter-example to save it rather than to test it.

## When to invoke it

- When a product or strategic hypothesis is presented as "confirmed" by data
- When a team cites successes without mentioning cases where it didn't work
- When a thesis is formulated so vaguely it couldn't be refuted
- When someone amends a theory at each counter-example to preserve it

## When not to invoke it

- When the hypothesis is too early-stage to be formulated in a testable way without emptying it of meaning
- When the goal is idea generation, not validation
- On value judgments that aren't falsifiable by nature

## Use cases

### 1. Product retention hypothesis

**Context:** A team claims their feature improves retention.

**Input:** "The data confirms that users who use feature X have better retention rates."

**What the skill produces:** "How would we know this hypothesis is false? — If it's correlation without causation, what would we observe? — What does it predict for users who don't use the feature? — Are there segments where this isn't true? — The most severe test: find users who use feature X and still churn. If you haven't looked for that case, you haven't tested the hypothesis."

### 2. Go-to-market strategy

**Context:** A team is "convinced" their target market is SMBs.

**Input:** "We're convinced SMBs are our market — all the customers we've signed are SMBs."

**What the skill produces:** "What would change your mind about this segment? — Is 'all signatures are SMBs' a test or a description of the current sales effort? — What's the falsifiable prediction? If SMBs are the right market, what should you observe in 6 months? — Have you looked for counter-examples: similar SMBs that didn't sign, larger companies that would have wanted to?"

### 3. Pricing hypothesis

**Context:** A team wants to raise prices and "thinks" elasticity is low.

**Input:** "We think our customers aren't very price-sensitive — they didn't react to past increases."

**What the skill produces:** "The hypothesis restated in falsifiable form: 'a 20% increase doesn't affect conversion rate or churn rate in the following 3 months.' — What would invalidate this hypothesis? — What was the magnitude of past increases? — Are there segments where elasticity might differ? — Provisional verdict: no refutation found for increases < X% in this segment. Not certainty."

## Limits of the encoding

Falsifiability requires the hypothesis to be formulated precisely enough to be testable — in social sciences and early-stage product, this precision is often impossible without emptying the hypothesis of meaning. The method says what hasn't been refuted yet, never what's true — which can be frustrating when you need a positive recommendation. And the confirmation/refutation asymmetry, while logically sound, is psychologically counter-intuitive: teams naturally resist looking for what would invalidate their thesis.
