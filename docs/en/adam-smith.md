# Adam Smith — The mechanisms that produce behaviors

## Why this encoding?

When an organizational or economic behavior doesn't match expectations, the first explanation is often moral: people are lazy, malicious, or incompetent. Smith encodes the inverse operation: refuse the intentional explanation and look for the mechanisms that rationally produce the observed behavior. A perverse effect is always a sign of a misaligned incentive — not of bad will.

## What the method corrects

When someone says "people aren't doing what we expect" or "we can't get teams aligned," the method asks: what does the system reward? What does it punish? The observed behavior is almost always rational given the incentives in place. Change the behavior = change the incentives. Not the speeches.

## When to invoke it

- When an organizational behavior doesn't match expectations and the first explanation is moral
- When a product policy generates unintended perverse effects
- When "we can't align teams" — often an incentive problem, not a communication one
- When a pricing or market structure decision produces unexpected behaviors

## When not to invoke it

- On behaviors with a strong cultural, ideological, or intrinsic explanation
- When motivations are explicitly non-economic and monetary incentives are minor
- For questions of meaning or belonging — Smith alone is insufficient

## Use cases

### 1. Team that doesn't document

**Context:** A tech team doesn't document its code despite repeated requests.

**Input:** "The team doesn't document — we keep asking but nothing changes."

**What the skill produces:** "Incentive map: what does the system reward? — Code reviews, deliverables, shipping speed. What does it ignore? — Documentation. What does it punish? — Slowness. — The observed behavior is rational: documentation takes time, is not rewarded, and slows down what is rewarded. — Change the behavior: integrate documentation into the definition of 'done,' or create protected time for it. Not another message about the importance of documentation."

### 2. Unexpected customer churn

**Context:** Customers are churning right after renewal, which wasn't anticipated.

**Input:** "We have a churn spike right after renewals — we don't understand why."

**What the skill produces:** "Incentive map on the sales side: what's rewarded in the renewal cycle? — The signed renewal. What's ignored? — Post-renewal customer satisfaction. — Mechanistic hypothesis: salespeople have an incentive to close at any cost, not to close genuinely satisfied customers. Result: renewals signed under pressure that generate churn shortly after. — Change the mechanism: incorporate 6-month retention into sales performance metrics."

### 3. Over-reporting in teams

**Context:** An organization is producing more and more reports that no one actually reads.

**Input:** "We produce more and more reports — but no one really reads them."

**What the skill produces:** "What does the system reward? — Producing and sending reports. What does it measure? — Number of reports produced, communication regularity. What doesn't it measure? — Whether reports are used for decisions. — Mechanism: producing a report is observable and traceable, its use isn't. The rational behavior is to produce to show activity. — Change the mechanism: tie reports to explicit decisions, or eliminate the ones that aren't read."

## Limits of the encoding

The method reduces behaviors to responses to economic incentives and underestimates intrinsic, cultural, or ideological motivations that can be equally powerful. Non-monetary incentives (status, belonging, meaning) are harder to model and modify. Changing incentives always produces unforeseen effects — the method anticipates this but doesn't eliminate it. Smith alone is insufficient on questions of power and ideology — combine with Marx on the same problems for a complete picture.
