# Montesquieu — The rule adapted to its context

## Why this encoding?

"It works at Spotify," "Amazon does it this way," "it's the industry standard" — importing models without analyzing their original context is one of the most frequent sources of poor organizational decisions. Montesquieu encodes the missing operation: verify whether the rule or model is adapted to the specific conditions of the context where you want to apply it. A rule that works in one context doesn't necessarily work in another.

## What the method corrects

When someone says "we apply the same rule everywhere" or "it works elsewhere so it'll work here," the method asks: what are the conditions that make it work there? Are those conditions present here? It corrects the copy-paste of models without analysis of their applicability context.

## When to invoke it

- When an organizational or governance model is imported from another context
- When a global rule is applied uniformly in different contexts
- To evaluate whether a sector best practice is transferable to a specific context
- When someone argues by example ("it works at X") without analyzing contextual differences

## When not to invoke it

- When the rule must apply uniformly for coherence or equity reasons — contextualism can become an argument against any uniformity
- On fast, low-stakes decisions
- When contexts are genuinely similar and adaptation isn't necessary

## Use cases

### 1. Importing the Spotify model

**Context:** A 30-person tech team wants to adopt Spotify's squad/tribe model.

**Input:** "Spotify revolutionized organization with squads — we should do the same."

**What the skill produces:** "Original context: Spotify developed this model with 1000+ engineers, a strong autonomy culture built over years, specific coordination systems. — Current context: 30 people, coordination still largely informal, culture being built. — Contextual presuppositions: the model assumes organizational maturity, formal coordination systems, and critical mass. — Are these conditions present? — Possible adaptation: draw on the principles (autonomy, ownership) without importing the full structure."

### 2. Applying a global HR policy

**Context:** An international group wants to apply the same performance review policy across all offices.

**Input:** "We're standardizing performance reviews — same process in all countries."

**What the skill produces:** "Was this rule designed for these contexts or in just one of them? — Contextual presuppositions: the performance review format assumes a certain relationship to hierarchy, transparency, and direct feedback. These assumptions vary strongly across cultures. — In which contexts does this rule work? What do they have in common? — Contexts where it risks producing contrary results? — Minimum necessary adaptations vs necessary uniformity."

### 3. Governance standard copied from a competitor

**Context:** A startup wants to adopt the governance model of an admired scale-up.

**Input:** "The governance of [admired scale-up] is very clear — we'll adopt their model."

**What the skill produces:** "What are the conditions that make this model work for them? — Size, history, ownership structure, transparency culture, team maturity. — Which of these conditions are present here? — What's transferable: the principles. What probably isn't: the formal structure designed for their specific context. — Build from the principles rather than importing the structure."

## Limits of the encoding

Contextualism can become an argument against any change or borrowing of practices: "our context is too specific" is sometimes a way to avoid being challenged. Comparing contexts requires actual knowledge of other contexts — without data, it becomes speculative. Risk of analytical paralysis: always finding a contextual specificity that justifies not deciding. And contrary to its intention, contextual reasoning can legitimize the status quo.
