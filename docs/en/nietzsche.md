# Nietzsche — The genealogy of values

## Why this encoding?

Mission statements, value declarations, and impact narratives share a common structure: they present as universal and disinterested what serves particular interests. Nietzsche encodes the operation of tracing these discourses back to their origin — who produced them, in what context, who benefits. The encoding is partial by nature: Nietzsche resists formalization, he goes in all directions. This skill encodes genealogy as a diagnostic method, not the whole of Nietzsche's philosophy.

## What the method corrects

When someone says "our mission is to help our customers" or "we're driven by our values," the method doesn't deny it — it asks: where does this discourse come from, who benefits from it, what does it mask? It corrects the face-value reading of value discourses in organizations — not out of cynicism, but because real interests often differ from declared interests.

## When to invoke it

- On corporate briefs where the mission is presented as pure and disinterested
- When a values discourse manifestly conceals unnamed interests
- On strategic decisions where the real motivations deserve to be named
- When someone uses "the common good" as an argument without defining it

## When not to invoke it

- On HR topics, vulnerability, or personal engagement — genealogical diagnosis can be harmful outside strategic contexts
- When you're looking for a course of action — Nietzsche exposes, he doesn't prescribe
- On low-stakes decisions where deconstruction is disproportionate

## Use cases

### 1. Corporate mission statement

**Context:** An e-commerce company presents its mission as "democratizing access to products."

**Input:** "Our mission: democratize access to quality products for everyone."

**What the skill produces:** "Where does this discourse come from? — Who benefits from 'democratizing access' being held as the real mission? — What does this discourse do concretely that 'maximize our market share' wouldn't? — What it probably masks: investor pressure, mechanisms for extracting value from third-party sellers, competitive positioning. — The direct question: what do you actually want?"

### 2. Diversity and inclusion policy

**Context:** A large company launches a D&I initiative with a strong communications apparatus.

**Input:** "We're launching a major D&I initiative — we genuinely believe diversity makes the company better."

**What the skill produces:** "Genealogy: when did this discourse appear? Under what external pressure — customers, regulators, press? — Who benefits from this initiative being visible? — What is actually different in recruitment, promotion, and compensation practices — not in communications? — Is the discourse built by direct affirmation or in reaction to criticism?"

### 3. "User value" framing for data collection

**Context:** A product team justifies a data-intensive feature through "user value."

**Input:** "We collect this data because we want to create value for our users — personalize their experience."

**What the skill produces:** "Who produced this 'user value' discourse in this context? — Does personalization primarily benefit the user or advertising revenue? — Did users explicitly ask for this personalization, or is this a retrospective construction? — The direct question: what do you actually want with this data?"

## Limits of the encoding

This skill is a partial approximation — Nietzsche resists formalization and can go in all directions. Universal demystification can slide into gratuitous cynicism: everything can be "deconstructed" if you look hard enough, to the point of leaving no value standing. The method exposes, it doesn't prescribe — it doesn't say what value to adopt in place of the one it's deconstructed. Do not invoke on HR topics or in contexts of vulnerability.
