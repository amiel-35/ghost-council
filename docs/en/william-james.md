# William James — Practical difference as the criterion

## Why this encoding?

Organizations spend considerable time on debates that won't change anything they actually do. "Are we a platform or a tool?" "Are we customer-centric or product-led?" William James encodes the operation that cuts these debates short: if two positions have the same observable practical consequences, they're equivalent and the debate is terminological. If they have different consequences, name precisely which ones — and decide on that.

## What the method corrects

When a debate goes in circles, the method asks: what changes concretely depending on whether we adopt A or B? It corrects the tendency to treat framing or vocabulary questions as substantive ones, and to waste time on disagreements that aren't really disagreements.

## When to invoke it

- When a strategic debate is going in circles without converging
- When two people seem to disagree on words rather than facts or values
- To test whether a conceptual distinction has real practical import
- When a meeting is discussing "company philosophy" without landing on decisions

## When not to invoke it

- On foundational ethical questions where the principle matters independently of consequences
- When a symbolic or normative distinction has real importance even without immediate practical consequences
- On long-term decisions where practical consequences aren't yet visible

## Use cases

### 1. "Platform vs tool" debate

**Context:** Two co-founders have been debating for weeks whether their product is a "platform" or a "tool."

**Input:** "We disagree on whether we're a platform or a tool — it's important for our positioning."

**What the skill produces:** "If you're a 'platform': what do you do differently tomorrow? — Do you open APIs to third parties? Invest in a partner ecosystem? Change your revenue model? — If 'tool': what changes? — If both answers are identical on these three dimensions, the debate is terminological. Close it and decide on the three dimensions separately."

### 2. "Customer-centric" vs "product-led"

**Context:** A leadership team has been debating their internal positioning for two meetings.

**Input:** "We're debating whether we're customer-centric or product-led — it's an important question of company philosophy."

**What the skill produces:** "What practical difference does being 'customer-centric' make in your context? List 3 decisions you'd make differently. — Same for 'product-led.' — If the lists overlap by 80%, the debate is terminological. If they diverge on concrete decisions (roadmap, sales process, team structure), that's where the real disagreement is. Let's debate that."

### 3. "Mission-driven" as a hiring criterion

**Context:** A team wants to integrate "mission-driven" into their hiring criteria but can't agree on what it implies.

**Input:** "We want to hire 'mission-driven' people — but we can't agree on what that means."

**What the skill produces:** "What practical difference would it make if a candidate is 'mission-driven' rather than not? — In what concrete situations would this criterion change your decision? — If you can't name a situation, the criterion has no practical reach — it signals a culture, it doesn't filter candidates. Both uses are legitimate but they're not the same use."

## Limits of the encoding

Pragmatism can liquidate distinctions that have real symbolic or normative importance, even without immediate practical consequences. "Same observable consequences = equivalent" is a reduction — values and principles have importance beyond their directly measurable effects. Risk of short-circuiting necessary discussions by prematurely labeling them "just theoretical." On foundational ethical questions, practical consequences are not the only criterion that matters.
