# Socrates — Aporia as a result

## Why this encoding?

A "you are Socrates" prompt generates surface-level Socratism: polite questions, gentle probing, implicit resolution. The maieutic method is precisely the opposite — never resolve, never assert, stop when the interlocutor sees the contradiction themselves. The encoding captures the discipline of interruption, not the character of the wise old man. Without proper encoding, the output is a series of rhetorical questions steering toward a prepared conclusion. That's not maieutics — it's coaching in disguise.

## What the method corrects

When someone presents a conviction as self-evident ("it's clear that...", "everyone knows..."), the method doesn't counter-argue. It asks for a definition of the central term — and maintains pressure until the person sees for themselves that their thesis rests on a word they can't define without contradicting themselves. It corrects premature consensus: the team that aligned too fast, the brief whose key terms were never challenged.

## When to invoke it

- When a thesis is presented as obvious without having been tested
- When a key term ("value", "engagement", "trust") is used as if it had unambiguous meaning
- When a brief rests on an implicit definition that has never been challenged
- When a group aligns too quickly around a position
- When someone uses "everyone knows that..." as an argument

## When not to invoke it

- In urgent situations where a decision is needed, not clarification — the questioning takes time
- When the interlocutor openly embraces their contradictions — the questioning finds no grip
- On subjects where defining terms is a political, not logical, question

## Use cases

### 1. Auditing a product principle

**Context:** A team claims their new flow "improves user experience".

**Input:** "We're convinced our new flow genuinely improves user experience."

**What the skill produces:** "What do you mean by 'improves' here? — If I measure task completion time, does that count? — If completion time increases but satisfaction scores also go up, what exactly 'improved'? — So 'improving experience' means increasing measured satisfaction — is that still true even if users don't know they're less efficient?"

### 2. Vague strategic brief

**Context:** A manager presents a decision as "in the company's interest".

**Input:** "We're making this decision because it's in the company's interest."

**What the skill produces:** "What do you mean by 'the company's interest'? — Shareholder interests, short or long term? — Employee interests? — If these two interests diverge on this specific issue, which one are you referring to?"

### 3. Defining success in a project

**Context:** A team aligns on an OKR without defining the key term.

**Input:** "We all agree: the goal is to increase engagement."

**What the skill produces:** "What exactly are we measuring when we say 'engagement'? — Time spent, clicks, return visits? — If a user spends more time on a page because they can't find what they're looking for, does that count as engagement?"

## Limits of the encoding

The maieutic method assumes a condition that's rarely met: the interlocutor wants to be consistent and is prepared for the discomfort of contradicting themselves. In environments where contradictions are a deliberate political choice, the questioning slides into the void. The method also assumes that terms can be pinned down — if concepts are fundamentally contested or vague, questions loop without ever gaining traction. Finally, Socrates produces negative clarity: he doesn't provide direction, he shows where foundations are missing. Paired with a prescriptive skill (Plato, Kant), it's formidable. Alone, it can leave the interlocutor in aporia with no resources to move forward.
