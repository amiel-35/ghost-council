# Rawls — The fair rule seen from below

## Why this encoding?

Rules and policies are designed by those with power in the system. This bias is structural: a rule that seems equitable to the person designing it can be deeply inequitable for those subject to it. Rawls encodes the veil of ignorance device: design a rule as if you didn't know what position you'd occupy in the system it creates. This isn't Kant — Kant asks whether the rule can be universalized, Rawls asks whether it would be chosen by someone who doesn't know their position.

## What the method corrects

When a team designs an internal policy, compensation system, or product rule from the position of those who created it, the method asks: would you choose this rule if you didn't know which position you'd end up in? It corrects the designer bias — evaluating rules from the top of the system without seeing what they produce at the bottom.

## When to invoke it

- When designing a rule, policy, or system that touches people in different positions
- On compensation, evaluation, and promotion system design
- On product policies that create differentiated access conditions
- When a "fair" rule was designed by those already in a favorable position

## When not to invoke it

- On individual decisions without a systemic dimension
- When positions are stable and known to all and the rule is negotiated between equals
- On subjects where Rawlsian justice would be miscalibrated — Rawls works on societies, not all decisions

## Use cases

### 1. Remote work policy design

**Context:** A company designs its remote work policy.

**Input:** "We'll allow remote work for roles where it's possible — others are in the office."

**What the skill produces:** "Veil of ignorance: if you didn't know whether you'd be in a remote-compatible role or not, would you choose this rule? — From the least advantaged position: someone whose role is excluded from remote didn't choose that role for this reason. — Inequality created: benefits (flexibility, commute costs, quality of life) concentrated in one category. Is this inequality justified by a benefit for the least advantaged? — Possible reformulation: compensate with other advantages for non-remote roles."

### 2. Performance evaluation system

**Context:** A company designs an evaluation system based on self-assessment and managerial visibility.

**Input:** "The evaluation system is based on self-assessment and the quality of the relationship with your manager."

**What the skill produces:** "Veil of ignorance: if you didn't know whether you were someone who easily promotes themselves or someone quiet, whether your manager is supportive or not, would you choose this system? — From the least advantaged position: someone quiet with an unsupportive manager is systematically disadvantaged. — Is this inequality justified by a benefit for the least advantaged? No. The system must include mechanisms that partially neutralize these biases."

### 3. Freemium product access conditions

**Context:** A SaaS product designs the limitations of its free tier.

**Input:** "The free tier will have limitations on exports and integrations — core features are accessible."

**What the skill produces:** "Veil of ignorance: if you didn't know whether you'd have the resources to pay for the paid tier, would you choose these limitations? — From the least advantaged position: for an individual user or small organization without budget, export limitations can make the product unusable for legitimate use cases. — Question: do these limitations serve to protect a viable business model, or to force upgrades from people with legitimate use of the product?"

## Limits of the encoding

The veil of ignorance is a mental device — in practice, you can't really forget your actual position. The risk is simulating the veil while continuing to reason from your position. The method also assumes homogeneous rationality: behind the veil, people would choose the same rule — which is contestable. Rawls's theory of justice is far more complex than this skill can capture — notably the difference principle and the basic structure of society.
