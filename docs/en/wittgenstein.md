# Wittgenstein — Dissolving problems through language clarity

## Why this encoding?

Most disagreements in organizations aren't about facts or values — they're about words being used differently by people who think they're talking about the same thing. "Innovation," "value," "engagement," "culture" are terms that slip between language games without anyone noticing. The Wittgenstein encoding produces a precise operation: spot the slip, stop it, anchor the term in its concrete use. Without this, briefs stay vague, meetings go in circles, and decisions rest on apparent consensus.

## What the method corrects

When someone says "we want to create value" or "the experience needs to be seamless," the method doesn't ask "what is value in general?" It asks: "In this sentence, in this context, what do you mean by that word?" It corrects pseudo-problems: disagreements that dissolve when you clarify terms, revealing that everyone agreed on the facts all along.

## When to invoke it

- When a brief contains abstract terms not defined in their concrete use
- When two people seem to disagree but are using the same word for different things
- When a meeting goes in circles without converging — often a sign of undetected semantic drift
- When a key term is used inconsistently within the same document

## When not to invoke it

- When the disagreement is substantive, not terminological — clarifying terms won't resolve a real conflict of interests
- In urgent situations where action takes priority over conceptual clarity
- On terms whose ambiguity is deliberate and functional (certain political or legal concepts)

## Use cases

### 1. Brief with "seamless user experience"

**Context:** A product brief calls for a "seamless and intuitive experience."

**Input:** "Primary goal: a seamless and intuitive user experience."

**What the skill produces:** "In this sentence, what does 'seamless' mean concretely? — Option A: few clicks to complete a task. Option B: no errors or blockers. Option C: subjective feeling of ease. — These three usages have very different design implications. Which one are you targeting? — Same question for 'intuitive': intuitive for whom, in what usage context?"

### 2. Disagreement over the company's "mission"

**Context:** Two co-founders have been debating whether their product is "a platform" or "a tool" for weeks.

**Input:** "We disagree: are we building a platform or a tool?"

**What the skill produces:** "In this debate, what do you mean by 'platform'? — And by 'tool'? — If I replace 'platform' with its concrete consequences: network effects, openness to third parties, transaction-based revenue model... — Does the disagreement concern the words or these consequences? If it's about consequences, the debate isn't terminological. If it's about the words, it is."

### 3. OKR with "increase engagement"

**Context:** A team defines an OKR around engagement without defining it.

**Input:** "KR: increase engagement by 20% in Q2."

**What the skill produces:** "What are we measuring when we say 'engagement' in this KR? — Sessions per week? Actions per session? Depth of feature usage? — These metrics aren't correlated — increasing one can decrease another. As long as 'engagement' isn't anchored to a precise metric, this KR is a pseudo-objective: you'll always be able to say you hit it or missed it depending on what you choose to measure after the fact."

## Limits of the encoding

This skill encodes the late Wittgenstein — of the Philosophical Investigations, not the Tractatus. Dissolving problems through term clarification can become a tool to avoid real substantive disagreements: "it's just a word problem" is sometimes a way not to face the real conflict. The method also requires enough usage context to anchor terms — without context, the clarification stays abstract. And over-clarifying risks getting bogged down in terminology rather than moving forward.
