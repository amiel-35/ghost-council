# Machiavelli — Power dynamics without the gloves

## Why this encoding?

Most stakeholder analyses are polite. You list the stakeholders, note their "concerns," look for alignment. Machiavelli encodes the honest analysis no one wants to do out loud: who actually wants what, who has the power to block or accelerate, and what happens when interests don't converge. This isn't amorality — it's seeing clearly what is, before deciding what you want to do about it.

## What the method corrects

When someone says "everyone is aligned" or "it's a consensus decision," the method asks: aligned on exactly what, and who actually wins in this deal? It corrects organizational naivety — the belief that decisions are made for the stated reasons, and that interests naturally converge when everyone is acting in good faith.

## When to invoke it

- Before any decision that creates unnamed winners and losers
- When "everyone is aligned" seems too good to be true
- In a negotiation where the real levers of each party haven't been mapped
- When an initiative has failed and you're looking for why — retrospective interest analysis is often revealing

## When not to invoke it

- In contexts where cooperation and trust are the scarce resources — power analysis can destroy them if surfaced
- On decisions where norms and legitimacy are real power resources the method underestimates
- When what you're trying to do is build a relationship, not analyze a power dynamic

## Use cases

### 1. Launching a cross-functional initiative

**Context:** A team wants to launch an initiative that touches several departments.

**Input:** "We're launching a cross-functional initiative — stakeholders are on board."

**What the skill produces:** "Map: who are the actors with power over this initiative? — What is each one actually trying to get or protect, not what they say? — Who loses something if the initiative succeeds? — Who can block? — 'On board' at what stage — in the meeting or in day-to-day execution? — Dependencies: who does each actor depend on? What's their lever over others?"

### 2. Negotiating with a strategic partner

**Context:** A startup negotiates a partnership with a large corporation.

**Input:** "We're negotiating a partnership — they seem very motivated to work with us."

**What the skill produces:** "What do they actually want from this partnership — not what they say? — Why us rather than someone else? — What do they gain if the partnership succeeds? If it fails? — What's our leverage? What do they lose if we don't sign? — Risk: if their real motivation is to observe us before copying or blocking us, what protects us?"

### 3. Internal restructuring decision

**Context:** Leadership considers a reorganization presented as beneficial for everyone.

**Input:** "The reorg will give each team more autonomy — it's in everyone's interest."

**What the skill produces:** "Who concretely gains from this reorg — more budget, more influence, less oversight? — Who loses? — Is 'autonomy' what the teams want or what leadership wants to give? — What threat stabilized the current situation, and what has destabilized it? — Recommendation: force or negotiated compromise? Based on who can block and at what cost."

## Limits of the encoding

Reducing behavior to interests can miss ideological or idealistic motivations that are sometimes real and effective — people sometimes do costly things because they genuinely believe in them. "Machiavellian" has become an insult: this skill encodes power analysis, not a license to amorality. The choice of how to act on this analysis remains normative and belongs to the decision-maker. The method also underestimates the value of norms and legitimacy as real, durable power resources.
