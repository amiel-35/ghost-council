# Darwin — What survives in this environment

## Why this encoding?

When you don't know which of several approaches will work, the reflex is to analyze in theory. Darwin encodes a different operation: map the selection pressures of the real environment to predict what's viable long-term. Not "which option is best in the abstract?" but "in this specific environment, with these constraints, which form has the best survival odds?"

## What the method corrects

When a team is weighing multiple approaches without certainty, the method doesn't ask "which is best?" but "what are the selection pressures, and which form is best adapted to them?" It also corrects teleology — the belief that what has survived until now is necessarily adapted to what's coming, or that evolution moves in a direction.

## When to invoke it

- When choosing between multiple approaches without certainty about which will work
- When the competitive environment is shifting and you want to predict what will remain viable
- In portfolio strategy: which initiatives deserve to be maintained, which abandoned
- When evaluating the durability of an existing model against environmental changes

## When not to invoke it

- On ethical decisions — "what survives" is not a moral norm
- When the environment is too unstable or new for selection pressures to be readable
- For short-term, low-stakes decisions

## Use cases

### 1. Choosing between distribution models

**Context:** A startup is weighing direct sales vs. reseller partners.

**Input:** "We're torn between selling directly or going through reseller partners — we don't know what will work."

**What the skill produces:** "What are the selection pressures in this market? — Who controls the customer relationship? — What speed of adoption is required? — What forms of distribution dominate among competitors, and why have they survived? — If the environment moves toward more consolidation, which of the two forms is better adapted? — Portfolio diversity: can we maintain both long enough to see what survives?"

### 2. Evaluating a revenue model

**Context:** A team evaluates whether their freemium model remains viable as the market evolves.

**Input:** "The market is shifting — we're wondering if our freemium model is still viable."

**What the skill produces:** "What are the current selection pressures: acquisition cost, willingness to pay, competition on the free tier? — Which models survive in similar markets that have evolved in this direction? — What was adapted yesterday (broad freemium) may become maladapted if infrastructure costs rise and willingness to pay stays flat. — Anticipate: if these trends continue, which currently marginal models would become dominant?"

### 3. Product initiative portfolio

**Context:** A team must prioritize among 5 parallel initiatives with limited resources.

**Input:** "We have 5 initiatives running — we no longer have the resources to run all of them."

**What the skill produces:** "What are the selection pressures in your context: market timing, measurable traction, resource constraints? — For each initiative: what selection mechanisms will decide whether it survives? — Which is best adapted to current pressures, not best in the abstract? — Portfolio diversity: if you cut too much, you lose the capacity to adapt to unexpected changes."

## Limits of the encoding

The Darwinian framework applied to social systems is a metaphor, not a mechanism — analogies can mislead if pushed too far. The method is descriptive and retrospective: it explains well what happened, less well what will happen in a rapidly changing environment. It ignores cooperative and symbiotic dynamics, which are also important adaptive mechanisms. And "variations" and "selection pressures" are terms difficult to delimit without starting from the conclusions in social contexts.
