# Plato — The ideal form behind the phenomenon

## Why this encoding?

"First principles" became a Silicon Valley buzzword, stripped of its normative dimension. Plato didn't reduce to fundamentals to optimize — he reduced to find the just form, with its ethical charge intact. The encoding reclaims this dimension: ascending from the concrete toward the universal, while asking what should be, not just what is. A "you are Plato" prompt generates philosophy-café content. This skill generates an operation: define by ascending abstraction, then ask the normative question.

## What the method corrects

When someone defines a concept by listing examples ("engagement means likes, comments, time spent..."), the method refuses. It asks what all these examples have in common — and pushes until a definition emerges that withstands counter-examples. It corrects the confusion between instance and essence, and the implicit refusal to ask the normative question: should this model exist, not just does it exist?

## When to invoke it

- When a definition is given as a list of examples rather than by essence
- When a team is optimizing an existing system without questioning whether that system should exist
- When a brief conflates description of reality with prescription of what's desirable
- When a decision is made "because that's how things work" without ascending to principles

## When not to invoke it

- When the domain is fundamentally variable (art, language, culture) — there's no stable form to find
- When ascending to abstraction would produce a definition too general to be actionable
- In early stage without data — the ascent to universals requires real cases to ascend from

## Use cases

### 1. Defining a product concept

**Context:** A team debates what "value" means in their product.

**Input:** "Value for our users is time savings, features, affordable pricing..."

**What the skill produces:** "These are instances — not a definition. What do time savings, features, and affordable pricing have in common? — None defines value alone. — Ascending: value is what resolves a real problem with less friction than the alternative. Does this definition exclude things you wanted to include? Does it include things that shouldn't be there?"

### 2. Auditing a monetization model

**Context:** A team examines different pricing models without questioning the model itself.

**Input:** "We looked at what exists: freemium, subscription, pay-per-use, enterprise..."

**What the skill produces:** "You've listed instances. Before choosing one: what's the ideal form of economic relationship with a user who derives value from your product? Not 'what works in the market' but: what would be just? Once we have the ideal form, we see which instance comes closest."

### 3. Organizational design

**Context:** A team designs their org chart by copying existing models.

**Input:** "We looked at how Spotify, Basecamp, Amazon do it — we'll draw inspiration from their models."

**What the skill produces:** "Before looking at what exists: what's the ideal organizational form for producing what you produce, with your specific constraints? Then we see if existing models approximate it — or whether you need something that doesn't exist yet."

## Limits of the encoding

Ascending to abstraction sometimes produces definitions too general to be actionable — "value is what solves a real problem" is true but barely operational. The normative dimension can become prescriptive without a mandate: Plato says what should be, which can conflict with real constraints teams can't ignore. On fundamentally contested domains (what is justice? beauty?), there's no stable form to find — the method spins in place.
