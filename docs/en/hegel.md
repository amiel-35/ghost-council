# Hegel — Transcending tension, not avoiding it

## Why this encoding?

Faced with two opposing positions, the default reflex is compromise — take a bit of each. Hegel encodes something different: find what each position sees correctly, and build a synthesis that transcends both at a higher level of abstraction. Not 50% of A and 50% of B. Something that neither A nor B could see from their position. Without this encoding, attempts to "find common ground" produce soft compromises that no one actually defends.

## What the method corrects

When a team is "split between two options" and looking for agreement, the method doesn't ask "what's the compromise?" It asks: what does each position see correctly, and where does each fall short? It corrects the false dichotomy presented as a forced choice, and the compromise that avoids choosing by giving a bit of each without giving coherence.

## When to invoke it

- When a team is stuck on two positions presented as mutually exclusive
- When a debate goes in circles without converging — a sign that both positions each have a piece of the truth
- When a proposed compromise leaves everyone unsatisfied
- When a tension in a system (growth vs stability, speed vs quality) seems unsolvable

## When not to invoke it

- When the two positions are genuinely incompatible and require a clear choice
- On concrete decisions with fixed constraints that make synthesis impossible in practice
- When time is short — the dialectic takes time to be rigorous

## Use cases

### 1. Speed vs quality debate

**Context:** A team is stuck between "ship fast" and "ship well."

**Input:** "On one hand we need to move fast, on the other we need to maintain quality — we can't decide."

**What the skill produces:** "Thesis — ship fast: what it sees correctly: speed of learning outweighs perfection in an uncertain market. Its limit: pushed to the extreme, it accumulates debt that slows things down later. — Antithesis — ship well: what it sees correctly: technical debt has a real cost on the capacity to iterate. Its limit: quality without shipping generates no real learning. — Synthesis: deliver smaller but complete slices, with differentiated quality criteria based on the reversibility of the decision."

### 2. Centralization vs team autonomy

**Context:** A debate on product governance between centralized teams and autonomous teams.

**Input:** "We're split: some want a centralized roadmap, others want each team to be autonomous."

**What the skill produces:** "Thesis — centralization: coherence, unified vision, avoids duplication. Limit: bottleneck, lacks local adaptability. — Antithesis — autonomy: speed, contextual adaptation, ownership. Limit: incoherence, coordination gaps. — Synthesis: subsidiarity principle — centralized decisions on what creates value through coherence (standards, interfaces), autonomous decisions on what creates value through local adaptation."

### 3. Build vs buy debate

**Context:** A tech team is debating whether to buy a tool or build it.

**Input:** "Some want to build our own solution, others want to buy — we're stuck."

**What the skill produces:** "Thesis — build: differentiation, control, exact fit. Limit: cost, time, maintenance. — Antithesis — buy: speed, proven reliability. Limit: dependency, coupling to a tool not designed for us. — Synthesis: buy for commodity (what doesn't differentiate), build for the differentiating core. The real question isn't build/buy but: what actually differentiates us?"

## Limits of the encoding

Forcing problems into the thesis/antithesis/synthesis schema can distort them when there's no real dialectical tension — some problems are simply choices, not tensions. "Transcending while preserving" (Aufhebung) is hard to operationalize without sliding into the compromise the method sought to avoid. And the synthesis can become a way to avoid choosing between genuinely incompatible positions, producing a more abstract stance that satisfies everyone without resolving the concrete problem.
