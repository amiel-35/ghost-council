# Sun Tzu — Asymmetry as a principle of action

## Why this encoding?

Attacking a stronger opponent head-on is rarely a winning strategy. Sun Tzu encodes the inverse operation: find where you're strong where the opponent is weak, and concentrate effort there. In competitive positioning with limited resources, this is the alternative to a war of attrition. The encoding extracts the method from The Art of War — mapping asymmetries and timing — without applying it literally to military combat.

## What the method corrects

When a team asks "how do we differentiate from a bigger competitor?", the default answer is a list of features or a better price. Sun Tzu asks first: where is the opponent weak? Where are you strong that they're not? The ideal victory is the one that requires no direct confrontation — defeating the opponent before the engagement by choosing the terrain.

## When to invoke it

- In competitive positioning against a larger or better-funded player
- When a team wants to attack a market head-on without analyzing the asymmetries
- To identify the optimal moment for an action (launch, acquisition, partnership)
- When resources are limited and effort must be concentrated on the most decisive point

## When not to invoke it

- In transparent or cooperative environments where deceptive strategy is costly
- On questions of the legitimacy of objectives — Sun Tzu says how to win, not whether it's worth it
- When the adversary frame is wrong — some markets aren't zero-sum games

## Use cases

### 1. Positioning against a market leader

**Context:** A startup competes against a dominant player with 10x the resources.

**Input:** "How do we differentiate from [market leader] who has 10x our resources?"

**What the skill produces:** "Map strengths and weaknesses: where is the leader strong? — Scale, brand, distribution, support budget. Where are they weak? — Slow decision-making, underserved segments, innovation stalled by legacy, impersonal customer relationships. — Where are you strong that they're not? — Vertical specialization, speed, direct relationships. — Decisive terrain: the segment where your strength meets their weakness. Don't attack their core — attack their unguarded flank."

### 2. Launching in a saturated market

**Context:** A team wants to launch a product in a market where 5 competitors are already established.

**Input:** "The market is crowded — how do we enter without getting crushed?"

**What the skill produces:** "Find the void: what segment, use case, or channel is left open by the 5 competitors? — The absent opponent matters as much as the present one. — Timing: what is the moment when the market is in motion — regulatory, technological, behavioral change — that creates favorable terrain for a new entrant? — Victory without confrontation: enter where no one is yet, not where everyone is fighting."

### 3. Acquiring a key partner

**Context:** A startup needs to sign a strategic partner before competitors do.

**Input:** "We need to sign this partner before our competitors do — how?"

**What the skill produces:** "Real power analysis at this specific point: what does the partner want that competitors can't offer? — This isn't a resource war if you find the right asymmetry. — Timing: when is the partner most receptive — before a contract renewal, after disappointment with a competitor? — What's the minimum needed to win here, not to win in general?"

## Limits of the encoding

The Art of War is a military text — the metaphors slip when applied to business without adjustment. "Avoiding direct confrontation" can become an excuse for inaction or avoidance of difficult decisions. The method works less well in transparent or cooperative environments where strategic concealment is costly. It says nothing about the legitimacy of objectives: it's a tool for winning, not for deciding whether it's worth fighting.
