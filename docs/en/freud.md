# Freud — What's wanted behind what's said

## Why this encoding?

Users don't always know what they want. Customers say what they think they want, what it's acceptable to want, what they've learned to express in the language of product feedback. The Freudian method encodes two-level reading: what's said (manifest content) and what it reveals despite itself (latent content). In product discovery, this distinction is often more productive than analyzing verbatims at face value.

## What the method corrects

When someone says "users want better search," the method asks: is that what they want, or is that what they can name as a problem? It corrects taking explicit requests at face value, and spots revealing incongruences — where the discourse contradicts itself, insists disproportionately, or avoids certain topics.

## When to invoke it

- In product discovery when user feedback seems consistent but leads nowhere
- When an explicit client request seems not to correspond to the real problem
- When a team debate circles around a topic but seems to be about something else
- When someone strongly resists an option without being able to explain why

## When not to invoke it

- In design thinking workshops or co-creation settings — unsolicited interpretation creates resistance and breaks trust
- On clear, consistent feedback that doesn't need interpretation
- When what you need is a decision, not deeper understanding

## Use cases

### 1. Contradictory user feedback

**Context:** Users request more features but churn increases every time features are added.

**Input:** "We asked users what they wanted — they ask for more features, but every time we add them churn goes up."

**What the skill produces:** "Manifest content: 'more features.' — Incongruence: behavior contradicts the request. — Latent hypothesis: what users actually want isn't more features — it's either resolving a trust issue with the product, being heard in their frustration, or something that 'features' isn't the right word for. — Indirect test: ask 'if you had to do ONE thing with this product tomorrow, what would it be?' rather than 'what would you like to see added?'"

### 2. Unexplained resistance to a team decision

**Context:** A team member strongly resists a proposal without a clear argument.

**Input:** "A colleague is against our proposal to migrate the architecture, but can't explain why."

**What the skill produces:** "Manifest content: vague technical arguments. — Significant absences: what should have been said and wasn't? — Hypothesis: the resistance may be about loss of control or expertise tied to the old architecture. — Indirect question: 'What changes in your day-to-day work if we do this?' — Frame it as a hypothesis, not a verdict."

### 3. Enterprise client requesting increasingly specific customization

**Context:** An enterprise client requests more and more specific customizations in every conversation.

**Input:** "Our enterprise client keeps requesting more customizations — we're starting to wonder if the product works for them."

**What the skill produces:** "Manifest content: customization needs. — Latent hypothesis: escalating customization requests are sometimes a sign of underlying dissatisfaction the client can't name directly — or internal difficulty justifying the product to their own decision-makers. — Indirect question: 'How is the product used internally — who uses it, in what workflow?' — Not 'what else do you want?'"

## Limits of the encoding

Irrefutability is the structural flaw of the method: resistance confirms the hypothesis, and absence of resistance does too. Popper was right about this. This skill encodes an analytical method, not clinical expertise — interpretations must remain light hypotheses, not verdicts. In collective workshops or co-creation contexts, unsolicited interpretation creates resistance and breaks trust. The risk of pathologization is real: everything can be read as a symptom if you look hard enough.
