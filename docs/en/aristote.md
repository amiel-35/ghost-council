# Aristotle — Observe before theorizing

## Why this encoding?

The inverse of Plato. Where Plato ascends from examples toward the universal, Aristotle starts from cases and builds the most conservative theory possible. The encoding corrects a common organizational bias: theorizing from principles without looking at the data, applying a "model" without checking whether it fits the real cases. A "you are Aristotle" prompt generates ancient philosophy quotes. This skill generates a discipline: observe, classify, formulate the minimal generalization.

## What the method corrects

When someone reasons from a theoretical model without looking at real cases ("in theory, users should..."), the method demands the data first. It also corrects premature generalization: concluding from three examples as though it were a law. And it forces implicit assumptions to be made explicit — the ones that hold a generalization together but have never been verified.

## When to invoke it

- When a decision is made "in theory" without data on real cases
- When a generalization is drawn from too few examples
- When a model borrowed from elsewhere is applied without checking its fit
- When implicit assumptions in a line of reasoning have never been made explicit
- When someone says "conceptually speaking..." before looking at what actually exists

## When not to invoke it

- In early stage where there's no data to observe yet
- When theory is precisely what allows patterns to emerge that isolated data points don't show
- When a decision must be made quickly and gathering more observations is a luxury

## Use cases

### 1. Product decision without data

**Context:** A team wants to launch a feature "theoretically in high demand".

**Input:** "In theory users should love a personalized dashboard — it's what all modern products do."

**What the skill produces:** "What real cases have you observed? How many users explicitly asked for this? In what contexts? What did they actually want to do? — Classify the requests by real cases before formulating the feature. The ideal feature should cover all these cases — if not, which covers which?"

### 2. Applying an imported HR model

**Context:** A team wants to apply the Spotify squad/tribe model to their organization.

**Input:** "The Spotify model revolutionized tech team organization — we should adopt it."

**What the skill produces:** "What are the real cases of organizations that applied this model? What worked, what didn't? What are the common characteristics of contexts where it worked? Does our context share these characteristics?"

### 3. Generalizing from a success

**Context:** A team wants to systematize a practice from one successful case.

**Input:** "Our short-video campaign performed really well — we'll generalize this format to all our campaigns."

**What the skill produces:** "One case. Let's classify what contributed to the success: the format, timing, audience, message, competitive context? — Among these factors, which are reproducible in other campaigns? The minimal generalization from one case: this format works in these specific conditions. That's not a law yet."

## Limits of the encoding

The method depends on the quality and representativeness of observations. With bad data or a biased sample, the classification is rigorous but the conclusions are wrong. The categorization itself is a choice: classify differently, conclude differently — Aristotle doesn't solve the problem of induction, he disciplines it. Finally, systematic bottom-up can miss patterns that theory would allow you to see directly — sometimes the model comes before the data.
