# Aristotle — Observe before theorizing

## Why this encoding?

The inverse of Plato. Where Plato ascends from examples toward the universal, Aristotle starts from cases and builds the most conservative theory possible. The encoding corrects a persistent organizational bias: theorizing from principles without looking at the data, applying a "model" without checking whether it fits the actual cases.

A "you are Aristotle" prompt generates ancient philosophy quotes. This skill generates a discipline: observe, classify, formulate the minimal generalization — and surface the implicit assumptions that hold the reasoning together without ever having been verified.

## What the method corrects

When someone reasons from a theoretical model without looking at real cases ("in theory, users should..."), the method demands the data first. When a generalization is drawn from three examples as though it were a law, the method forces a naming of how many cases were observed and under what conditions. When implicit assumptions in a line of reasoning have never been made explicit, the method brings them to the surface before proceeding.

## When to invoke it

- When a decision is made "in theory" without data on real cases
- When a generalization is drawn from too few examples
- When a model borrowed from elsewhere is applied without checking whether the context fits
- When implicit assumptions in a line of reasoning have never been made explicit
- When someone says "conceptually speaking..." before looking at what actually exists

## When not to invoke it

- In early stage where there's no data to observe yet — the method stalls without material
- When theory is precisely what allows patterns to emerge that isolated data points don't show
- When a decision must be made quickly and gathering more observations is a luxury the situation doesn't allow

## Use cases

### 1. Product decision from "everyone wants this"

**Context:** A team wants to prioritize a feature because it "seems obvious" and "all competitors have it."

**Input:** "In theory users should love a personalized dashboard — it's what all modern products do."

**What the skill produces:** "What concrete cases have you observed? How many users explicitly asked for this, and in what context? What did they actually want to do — personalize what, to accomplish what? Classify the cases before formulating the feature. If the cases are heterogeneous, which does the feature cover and which does it leave out?"

### 2. Applying an imported organizational model

**Context:** Leadership wants to adopt the Spotify squad/tribe model after reading an article about it.

**Input:** "The Spotify model revolutionized tech team organization — we should adopt it."

**What the skill produces:** "What are the real cases of organizations that applied it — not the published success stories, the full cases? What worked, what didn't go as expected? What contextual characteristics are common to cases where it succeeded? Does our context share those characteristics, or are the conditions structurally different on key points?"

### 3. Generalizing from a success case

**Context:** A team wants to systematize a practice from one campaign that performed well.

**Input:** "Our short-video campaign blew up — we're generalizing this format to all our campaigns."

**What the skill produces:** "One case. Let's classify what contributed to the success: the video format, the timing, the specific audience, the message, the competitive context that month? Among these factors, which are reproducible across other campaigns, and which were specific to this case? Minimal generalization: this format worked under these specific conditions. Before generalizing, test on two different cases to see what holds."

## Limits of the encoding

The method depends entirely on the quality and representativeness of the available observations. With biased data or too small a sample, the classification is rigorous but the conclusions are wrong — and the rigor of the form can create an appearance of solidity the substance doesn't have.

The categorization itself is a choice that shapes the conclusion: classify the same cases differently and you get different theories. Aristotle disciplines induction; he doesn't solve it.

Finally, systematic bottom-up has a blind spot: sometimes theory is what allows you to see patterns that isolated data points don't reveal. A biologist without evolutionary theory classifies species — he doesn't see natural selection. The method is an empirical discipline, not a guarantee against theoretical blindness.
