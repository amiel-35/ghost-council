# Epictetus — What is in your control, nothing else

## Why this encoding?

Post-mortems and strategic discussions under constraint share a common failure: energy is spent on what can't be controlled. The market, the competitor's move, the budget that wasn't approved, the incoming regulation. Epictetus encodes a sorting operation: cut what's not in your control from the reasoning, and concentrate all effort on what is. Not as resignation — as discipline.

## What the method corrects

When someone says "the problem is the market" or "we depend on their decision," the method doesn't deny these constraints exist. It asks: is this in your control? If not, we stop discussing it and move to what is. It also corrects pseudo-constraints — what's presented as beyond your control but actually isn't. Difficult ≠ impossible.

## When to invoke it

- In post-mortems when the debrief circles around external factors
- When a team is paralyzed by constraints they don't control
- In planning under strong constraint to identify what's actionable
- When "there's nothing we can do" is used as a conclusion too quickly

## When not to invoke it

- In contexts where influencing what's "beyond your control" is precisely the work (advocacy, lobbying, negotiation)
- On questions of real structural injustice — "it's out of your control" can naturalize what's political
- When understanding the external context is necessary to make a good decision

## Use cases

### 1. Post-mortem of a failed launch

**Context:** A product launch failed and the team is doing a debrief.

**Input:** "The launch failed because of timing — we were in the middle of an economic downturn, customers weren't spending."

**What the skill produces:** "Two columns: out of your control (the economic downturn, customer behavior, macro timing) / in your control (the choice of launch timing, validating traction before investing in launch, the ability to detect weak signals earlier). — The downturn is named, then dropped. No more commentary on it. All recommendations address the second column."

### 2. Conversation stuck on competition

**Context:** A team is discussing how to react to a competitor's move.

**Input:** "Since the competitor lowered prices, there's nothing we can do — customers are leaving."

**What the skill produces:** "What's in your control? — The decision to lower prices or not. How you communicate your differentiation. Segments where price sensitivity is low. Strengthening relationships with current customers. — What's out of your control? — The competitor's pricing decision. The behavior of price-sensitive customers. — The competitor's decision is named, then dropped. No more recommendations on it."

### 3. Planning under budget constraint

**Context:** A team must produce a roadmap with 30% less budget.

**Input:** "With 30% less budget, we can't deliver what we planned — we're stuck."

**What the skill produces:** "Out of your control: the budget decision. In your control: what we choose to deliver with this budget, the prioritization criteria, the communication about what won't be done and why, the proposal to revisit the budget conversation if traction justifies it. — 'We're stuck' is a pseudo-constraint if you haven't yet decided what to cut. Decide what to cut."

## Limits of the encoding

The dichotomy is sometimes too binary: some things are partially within our control, some constraints can be influenced even if we don't fully control them. The method can seem indifferent to real structural injustices — the distinction doesn't neutralize causes, it orients action. Risk of resigned stoicism if misapplied: "it's out of my control" as an excuse rather than a clarification. In contexts where influencing what's "beyond your control" is precisely the work, the method is ill-suited.
