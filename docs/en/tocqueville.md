# Tocqueville — Tensions that accumulate before they explode

## Why this encoding?

Systems don't collapse suddenly — they accumulate tensions for a long time before the breaking point. The decision that "triggered everything" is almost never the real cause. Tocqueville encodes the reading of these structural tensions and their long-term trajectories. This isn't forecasting — it's identifying the forces opposing each other in a system and tracing where they lead if they continue.

## What the method corrects

When someone says "things are fine for now" or "the situation is stable," the method asks: what are the structural tensions under the surface, and where do they lead in 5-10 years? It corrects the confusion between surface stability and long-term viability — a system can be stable and accumulating tensions that will make this stability unsustainable.

## When to invoke it

- On long-term organizational or technical architecture decisions
- When a situation is presented as stable without analysis of underlying tensions
- To evaluate whether a decision accelerates or slows existing structural tensions
- On long-term strategy topics where trajectories matter more than current state

## When not to invoke it

- On short-term operational decisions where long-term trajectory is beside the point
- When you need an immediate recommendation, not a trajectory analysis
- On topics evolving too rapidly for long-term trajectories to be readable

## Use cases

### 1. Growth model based on low pricing

**Context:** A company has built its growth on very low prices and massive acquisition.

**Input:** "Our model is working great — strong growth, good retention."

**What the skill produces:** "Forces in tension: (1) margin pressure to maintain growth vs (2) need to invest in product and support to maintain retention. — Trajectory: if both forces continue, in 3-5 years either margins collapse, or quality degrades and retention follows. — Likely breaking point: when acquisition cost exceeds LTV, or when a competitor with a healthier economic model enters the market. — Does the current decision accelerate or slow this tension?"

### 2. Organization depending on founders for decisions

**Context:** A scale-up still has founders involved in all major decisions.

**Input:** "Our organization works well — founders are involved and teams are aligned."

**What the skill produces:** "Structural tension: founders' bandwidth is fixed, organizational complexity grows. — Trajectory: as the organization scales, either founders become a bottleneck, or they delegate without having built autonomous decision-making systems. — Breaking point: when growth speed exceeds founders' coordination capacity. — Question: what decisions today build the organization's decision-making capacity independent of founders?"

### 3. Technical debt in a critical system

**Context:** A tech team manages growing technical debt with no repayment plan.

**Input:** "Technical debt is manageable — we're still shipping."

**What the skill produces:** "Structural tension: required shipping speed maintains or grows debt, growing debt reduces future shipping speed. — Trajectory: by continuously deferring, the repayment cost exceeds the team's capacity to do it without stopping almost completely. — Likely breaking point: a major incident or regulatory change that forces urgent intervention in highly degraded code. — The decision not to address it now: to what extent does it accelerate the tension?"

## Limits of the encoding

Long-term predictions are inherently uncertain — the method identifies probable trajectories, not certainties. It can produce systematic pessimism: always finding tensions that will lead to a crisis. The method requires solid knowledge of the structural dynamics of the domain — without it, the identified tensions are superficial. Risk of paralysis: if every decision is seen in its long-term trajectory, none seems inconsequential.
