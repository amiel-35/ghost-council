# Sartre — Bad faith named

## Why this encoding?

"We had no choice," "the situation imposes it," "everyone does it," "we're forced to" — these formulations appear in nearly every brief and nearly every meeting. Sartre encodes the operation of turning them over: you had a choice, you chose this, and the cost of the alternative was the real reason, not the constraint. This isn't accusation — it's naming clearly what's at stake. This skill is the only one in the ghost-council capable of recommending that you abandon the project.

## What the method corrects

Bad faith is a precise mechanism: disguising your own choices as suffered constraints to avoid taking responsibility for them. "We have no choice" is almost always false — there is a choice, with its costs. The method reverses the formulation: "we're choosing X because the alternative would cost Y and we don't want to pay that price." More honest, and often a useful piece of information.

## When to invoke it

- When a decision is presented as a suffered constraint rather than an owned choice
- When "we have no choice" appears multiple times in the same brief
- When a team seems to be avoiding naming the real reason for a decision
- When a project seems to exist to avoid facing a deeper decision

## When not to invoke it

- On low-stakes decisions — the method is disproportionate
- Monday morning before coffee — existential radicalism requires the right context
- When structural constraints are real and framing them as "choices" would be a guilt-inducing injunction

## Use cases

### 1. Pivot presented as inevitable

**Context:** A team presents a strategic pivot as if it were imposed by the market.

**Input:** "We have no choice — the market is forcing us to pivot to enterprise."

**What the skill produces:** "Reversal: you have a choice. What would the alternative — staying on SMB — cost? — Likely: slower growth, less favorable unit economics, difficulty raising. — Honest reformulation: 'We're choosing to pivot to enterprise because staying on SMB would lead us to a dead end in 18 months, and that cost is higher than the cost of the pivot.' That's a real decision. Owning it changes how you execute it."

### 2. Layoffs dressed as "restructuring"

**Context:** Leadership presents layoffs as an unavoidable economic necessity.

**Input:** "We're forced to reduce headcount — the economic situation leaves us no other choice."

**What the skill produces:** "What alternatives were explored — temporary salary reductions, stopping certain investments, emergency fundraising? If none, that means the cost of those alternatives was judged higher than the cost of layoffs. Reformulation: 'We're choosing layoffs because the alternatives would cost X and we don't want to pay that price.' Naming the choice changes the moral responsibility of the decision."

### 3. Project existing to avoid a decision

**Context:** A "digital transformation" project has been running without delivering for 18 months.

**Input:** "We're continuing the digital transformation — it's a critical initiative."

**What the skill produces:** "Existential conclusion mode: does this project exist to produce something, or to avoid facing a deeper decision — like the fact that the core business model is no longer viable? If the project is a way to avoid the real decision, continuing is bad faith. The recommendation: name the real decision the project is allowing you to avoid, and face it directly."

## Limits of the encoding

The radicalism of Sartrean freedom is contested: structural constraints are real and don't entirely reduce to individual choices. Saying "you had a choice" to someone under strong institutional constraint can become a guilt-inducing injunction rather than an analysis. The existential conclusion (recommending abandoning the project) is powerful but hard to calibrate — reserve it for situations where it's genuinely warranted, not as a default posture. Bad faith is a concept that's hard to operationalize without over-interpreting.
